# Nonperturbative Hadron Mass Fractions: A QCD-Focused Conceptual EFT Note

## Abstract

This note reformulates the original broad motivation away from claims of new gravitational dynamics and toward a narrower, physically testable question: how much of the hadron mass budget of a bound state should be understood as nonperturbative infrared QCD structure rather than as a sum of current-particle masses? In this restricted setting, the framework becomes a conceptual EFT language for emergent hadron mass rather than a theory of gravity.

The central proposal is to define, for a hadron `H`, an effective residual mass component `M_\Omega^{(H)}` and a corresponding fraction `f_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F]` that isolate the non-current-mass share of the hadron mass relative to a chosen explicit flavor sector `\mathcal F`. This quantity can be connected to standard QCD objects: the energy-momentum tensor, the trace anomaly, Feynman-Hellmann sigma terms, lattice-QCD mass decompositions, and near-threshold quarkonium production. The note introduces one concrete effective parameter for phenomenology,

```math
f_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F] = 1 - \frac{1}{M_H}\sum_{q\in\mathcal F} \sigma_{qH},
```

where `\sigma_{qH}` are the standard sigma terms. The point is not that this formula proves a new theory of mass, but that it identifies a narrow observable handle by which a residual-mass language can be connected to nonperturbative QCD. By construction, `M_\Omega` is a bookkeeping remainder: it collects everything not explicitly assigned to the chosen current-mass sector.

---

## 1. Scope and motivation

The strongest physically meaningful core of the original motivation is not vacuum subtraction by itself. That idea is standard. The nontrivial idea is narrower:

> observable hadron mass may be dominated by localized nonperturbative QCD energy rather than by the sum of microscopic current masses.

This statement is already close to established hadron physics. For light hadrons, most of the mass does not come from the bare up- and down-quark masses. It arises from confinement, gluonic field energy, chiral symmetry breaking, and the QCD trace anomaly. Therefore a QCD-focused reformulation is scientifically more realistic than a claim about new gravity.

This note should thus be read as a conceptual EFT framework for emergent hadron mass in strongly interacting bound states.

---

## 2. Standard QCD anchor

The correct starting point is the QCD energy-momentum tensor and its trace. At the operator level,

```math
T^{\mu}{}_{\mu}
=
\frac{\beta(g)}{2g}
G^a_{\alpha\beta}G^{a\alpha\beta}
+
\bigl(1+\gamma_m(g)\bigr)
\sum_q m_q\,\bar q q.
```

This formula is standard and immediately shows why hadron mass is not simply a sum of current quark masses. Even in the chiral limit, the gluonic term remains. Therefore the trace anomaly and nonperturbative gluodynamics are not optional decorations; they are part of the standard QCD mass budget.

For a one-hadron state `|H(P)\rangle`, the forward matrix element of the energy-momentum tensor determines the hadron mass scale. In the hadron rest frame, the mass is encoded in expectation values of QCD energy densities rather than in a naive sum of constituent rest masses.

The conceptual shift proposed here is therefore modest but useful:

- not “mass comes from nothing,”
- but “the dominant part of hadron mass is generated by localized nonperturbative confinement-scale structure.”

---

## 3. From current-mass contribution to residual non-current mass

The cleanest operational split uses the Feynman-Hellmann theorem. For a hadron `H`, define the standard sigma terms

```math
\sigma_{qH} = m_q\,\frac{\partial M_H}{\partial m_q}.
```

The sum

```math
M_{\mathrm{current}}^{(H)} \equiv \sum_q \sigma_{qH}
```

captures the piece of the hadron mass directly attributable to explicit quark mass parameters.

This motivates the definition

```math
M_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F] \equiv M_H - \sum_{q\in\mathcal F} \sigma_{qH}.
```

This is the effective residual non-current-mass contribution of the hadron. Here the symbol `\Omega` is only a label for that residual sector; it is not meant to represent a new conserved charge or a new microscopic field. The definition is an EFT-level decomposition of the observed hadron mass into:

1. an explicit current-mass part,
2. a residual structured part generated by confinement, gluonic energy, condensate physics, and the trace anomaly.

Equally importantly, the definition is residual by construction: all contributions not explicitly assigned to the chosen current-mass sector are grouped into `M_\Omega`. In that sense, `M_\Omega` is a bookkeeping functional rather than a fundamental operator identity.

The corresponding dimensionless observable handle is

```math
f_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F] = \frac{M_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F]}{M_H} = 1 - \frac{1}{M_H}\sum_{q\in\mathcal F} \sigma_{qH}.
```

For light hadrons, one expects `f_\Omega^{(H)}` to be close to unity, precisely because current quark masses contribute only a small fraction of the proton or neutron mass.

This is the most important shift in the whole program: `M_\Omega` is no longer introduced by dividing an arbitrary energy by `c^2`; it is anchored to a standard QCD mass-budget decomposition.

---

## 4. Trace-anomaly fraction as a second observable handle

The previous definition isolates the non-current-mass share, but it does not separate gluonic field energy from the trace anomaly. A more refined quantity is the anomaly fraction

```math
f_{\mathrm{anom}}^{(H)}
\equiv
\frac{
\langle H|\frac{\beta(g)}{2g}G^a_{\alpha\beta}G^{a\alpha\beta}|H\rangle
}{
\langle H|T^{\mu}{}_{\mu}|H\rangle
}.
```

This parameter is conceptually valuable because it points to a more specific physical question:

> what fraction of the hadron mass scale is carried by the anomalous breaking of classical scale invariance in QCD?

That question is not metaphysical. It is connected to mainstream hadron structure physics and, at least in principle, can be constrained by lattice QCD and by experimental probes sensitive to the scalar gluon content of the nucleon.

Thus the residual-mass language becomes potentially useful if it is interpreted as a language for anomaly- and confinement-generated mass fractions, rather than as a substitute for the Standard Model.

---

## 5. Relation to Ji-style mass decomposition

Modern hadron structure theory often writes the hadron mass budget schematically as

```math
M_H = M_H^{\mathrm{q,kin}} + M_H^{\mathrm{g}} + M_H^{\mathrm{q,mass}} + M_H^{\mathrm{anom}}.
```

The precise separation is renormalization-scheme dependent, but the conceptual content is stable: hadron mass is distributed among quark kinetic energy, gluon field energy, explicit quark masses, and anomaly-related terms.

Within that language, the present note proposes the effective identification

```math
M_\Omega^{(H)} \sim M_H^{\mathrm{g}} + M_H^{\mathrm{anom}} + M_H^{\mathrm{conf}},
```

where `M_H^{\mathrm{conf}}` is not a separately conserved operator but a shorthand for the structured nonperturbative binding sector that is not reducible to explicit current masses.

This is intentionally a phenomenological identification, not a theorem. Its merit depends on whether it organizes measurable or calculable hadron-structure information more effectively than standard language.

### 5.1. Renormalization-scale and operator status

The quantity `f_\Omega^{(H)}` should not be overstated. The sigma terms `\sigma_{qH}` defined through the Feynman-Hellmann theorem are physically meaningful hadronic mass derivatives, but the further resolution of the residual piece into quark-kinetic, gluonic, anomalous, and confinement-like subcomponents is renormalization-scale and scheme dependent.

Accordingly, it is useful to distinguish two levels of statement.

1. The residual mass-budget parameter

```math
M_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal{F}] = M_H - \sum_{q\in\mathcal{F}} \sigma_{qH}
```

depends on the chosen explicit flavor set `\mathcal{F}` and, when heavy flavors are included through matching, on the associated convention.

If one wants to compare this residual parameter with a Ji-style operator decomposition at a definite renormalization point, it is useful to display that extra bookkeeping explicitly, for example as `M_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F;\mu,\mathcal S]` or `f_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F;\mu,\mathcal S]`, where `\mu` is the renormalization scale and `\mathcal S` the operator basis or matching prescription. The point of this notation is not that the hadron mass itself depends on `\mu`, but that comparisons between the residual bucket and a resolved operator decomposition only make sense within a fixed prescription.

2. The stronger identification

```math
M_\Omega^{(H)} \sim M_H^{\mathrm g} + M_H^{\mathrm{anom}} + M_H^{\mathrm{conf}}
```

is only a phenomenological bookkeeping statement inside a chosen decomposition scheme.

Therefore `M_\Omega^{(H)}` is not a Noether charge, not a conserved current, and not a unique operator-level invariant. It is best understood as an EFT parameter that summarizes the non-current-mass share of the hadron mass budget.

### 5.2. What this adds beyond Ji decomposition

The natural reviewer objection is immediate: why not just use the standard Ji decomposition directly? The answer is that the present construction does not replace Ji's operator analysis; it compresses one particular question into a simpler EFT summary variable.

The distinction is the following.

1. Ji-style decompositions resolve the hadron mass budget into several operator sectors at a chosen renormalization scale and in a chosen scheme.
2. `f_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F]` collapses that information to a single dimensionless measure of how suppressed the explicit current-mass sector is relative to the full hadron mass.
3. Because it is defined directly from sigma terms, `f_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F]` can be compared across hadrons even when a full operator-level decomposition is not available.
4. The same bookkeeping also extends naturally to in-medium phenomenology through `\kappa_\Omega(n_B)`, which is harder to phrase directly in the unreduced operator-decomposition language.

So the added value is organizational rather than foundational. `f_\Omega` is useful if it makes cross-hadron comparison, lattice-to-phenomenology translation, or dense-matter modeling cleaner than the unreduced decomposition. If it does not, then it should be regarded as notation rather than as new physics.

## 6. Bag-model intuition and why it remains useful

The MIT bag model is not fundamental QCD, but it remains pedagogically important because it already encodes the relevant intuition:

```math
M_H \sim E_{\mathrm{kin}} + B V + \cdots,
```

where `B` is the bag constant and `BV` is the bag-pressure energy cost associated with carving out a localized hadronic region from the surrounding QCD ground state.

The present framework does not rely on the bag model as literal microscopic truth. Instead it extracts the robust lesson:

localized bound-state mass can naturally be interpreted as energy stored in a nonperturbative confinement-scale region.

That is exactly the type of physical picture the `M_\Omega` functional is trying to capture, but now tied to modern QCD language rather than to abstract background-energy rhetoric.

## 7. Observable handles

The framework becomes scientifically useful only if at least one quantity can be tied to observation or computation.

### 7.1. A first nucleon estimate

For the nucleon, the cleanest semi-quantitative estimate uses standard sigma terms. Taking the explicit sector to be the light and strange sigma terms plus a small matched heavy-quark remainder, write

```math
M_\Omega^{(N)}[\mathcal F_N] = M_N - \sum_{q\in\mathcal F_N} \sigma_{qN},
\qquad
f_\Omega^{(N)}[\mathcal F_N] = 1 - \frac{1}{M_N}\sum_{q\in\mathcal F_N} \sigma_{qN}.
```

Using a lattice-motivated central estimate

```math
\sigma_{\pi N} \equiv \sigma_{uN} + \sigma_{dN} \approx 44\ \mathrm{MeV},
\qquad
\sigma_{sN} \approx 30\ \mathrm{MeV},
```

and allowing a small additional heavy-quark contribution at the level of a few MeV, one obtains the order-of-magnitude total

```math
\sum_q \sigma_{qN} \approx 80 \pm 15\ \mathrm{MeV}.
```

With `M_N \approx 939\ \mathrm{MeV}`, this gives

```math
f_\Omega^{(N)}
\approx
1 - \frac{80\pm 15}{939}
\approx
0.91 \pm 0.02.
```

Equivalently, the explicit current-mass contribution to the nucleon mass is at the level of roughly `8\%` to `10\%`, while the remaining `90\%` is naturally assigned to structured nonperturbative QCD dynamics in the present language.

If one uses a deliberately broader literature range,

```math
\sigma_{\pi N} \sim 42\text{--}60\ \mathrm{MeV},
\qquad
\sigma_{sN} \sim 25\text{--}40\ \mathrm{MeV},
```

then the estimate relaxes to roughly

```math
f_\Omega^{(N)} \sim 0.89\text{--}0.93.
```

This is already a useful result. It shows that the residual non-current-mass fraction for the nucleon is not an abstract philosophical label; it is numerically close to unity for standard QCD reasons that are independently studied in lattice and hadron-structure physics.

At the same time, the result should be read with the appropriate caveat. The quantity `f_\Omega^{(N)}[\mathcal F_N]` is stable as a low-energy mass-budget parameter only after one specifies which sigma terms are treated as explicit and which residual contributions are absorbed into the non-current-mass sector. It is therefore a useful EFT summary variable, not a fundamental scheme-free observable in the same sense as the full nucleon mass itself.

### 7.2. Lattice-QCD mass decomposition

The first handle is straightforward. Lattice QCD and hadron structure calculations can estimate:

- sigma terms `\sigma_{qH}`;
- gluonic energy fractions;
- trace-anomaly contributions.

From these, one may infer `f_\Omega^{(H)}` and `f_{\mathrm{anom}}^{(H)}` for the nucleon and other hadrons. If these quantities show stable qualitative patterns across renormalization schemes, flavor conventions, and hadron classes, the formalism gains substance.

### 7.3. Near-threshold quarkonium production

Near-threshold `J/\psi` photoproduction and related processes are sensitive to the scalar gluon structure of the nucleon. Therefore they provide a possible empirical window into the anomaly-dominated part of the mass budget.

In the present language, that means `f_{\mathrm{anom}}^{(N)}` is not just a formal symbol; it is at least indirectly connected to measurable amplitudes.

### 7.4. Dense matter and neutron-star EOS

If the non-current-mass part of the hadron budget changes materially in dense baryonic matter, then it is natural to ask how that contribution shifts with density. This motivates an effective dense-matter response parameter:

```math
\kappa_\Omega(n_B)
\equiv
\frac{d\ln M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)}{d\ln n_B},
```

where `n_B` is baryon density.

This is not yet a derived quantity. It is a proposed EFT parameter for neutron-star and compact-matter phenomenology. If it were calculable in dense-QCD-inspired models, it could feed into EOS corrections, tidal deformabilities, and the onset of deconfinement.

That makes `\kappa_\Omega` a realistic candidate for a “new effective parameter” rather than a purely philosophical slogan.

### 7.5. A minimal toy EOS embedding

To turn `\kappa_\Omega(n_B)` into a falsifiable object, one can embed it into a reference equation of state that is already causal and already compatible with the observed existence of `2M_\odot` neutron stars. Let `\epsilon_{\mathrm{ref}}(n_B)` and `P_{\mathrm{ref}}(n_B)` denote such a reference EOS, and let `n_{\mathrm{ref}}` be a matching density, for example of order nuclear saturation density.

Define the density-dependent residual-mass shift

```math
\Delta M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)
\equiv
M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)-M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_{\mathrm{ref}}).
```

The simplest embedding is then

```math
\epsilon(n_B)
=
\epsilon_{\mathrm{ref}}(n_B)
+
n_B\,\Delta M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B),
```

```math
P(n_B)
=
P_{\mathrm{ref}}(n_B)
+
n_B^2\frac{d\,\Delta M_\Omega^{(N)}}{dn_B}
=
P_{\mathrm{ref}}(n_B)
+
n_B M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)\,\kappa_\Omega(n_B),
```

where the last equality follows from

```math
\frac{dM_\Omega^{(N)}}{dn_B}
=
\frac{M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)}{n_B}\,\kappa_\Omega(n_B).
```

This already shows the basic physics. If `\kappa_\Omega<0`, the residual sector decreases with density and tends to soften the EOS. If `\kappa_\Omega>0`, it stiffens the EOS. The corresponding contribution to the adiabatic sound speed is obtained from

```math
c_s^2
=
\frac{dP/dn_B}{d\epsilon/dn_B}
=
\frac{
P_{\mathrm{ref}}'(n_B)
+
M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)\,\kappa_\Omega(n_B)\bigl(1+\kappa_\Omega(n_B)\bigr)
+
n_B M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)\,\kappa_\Omega'(n_B)
}{
\epsilon_{\mathrm{ref}}'(n_B)
+
\Delta M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)
+
M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)\,\kappa_\Omega(n_B)
},
```

where primes denote derivatives with respect to `n_B`. This toy model is already sufficient to propagate the residual-sector hypothesis into neutron-star observables such as `M-R` curves, tidal deformabilities, and the high-density sound-speed profile.

### 7.6. A bounded two-parameter ansatz for `\kappa_\Omega`

An unbounded power-law choice for `\kappa_\Omega(n_B)` is too dangerous because it can drive an uncontrolled exponential suppression of the residual sector. A better two-parameter ansatz is the bounded form

```math
\kappa_\Omega(n_B)
=
-\kappa_\infty
\frac{\left(n_B/n_0\right)^\nu}{1+\left(n_B/n_0\right)^\nu},
\qquad
0<\kappa_\infty<1,
\quad
\nu>0,
```

where `n_0` is the fixed nuclear saturation density and the two free parameters are `\kappa_\infty` and `\nu`.

This choice has three useful properties.

1. `\kappa_\Omega(n_B)\to 0` at low density, so the new effect switches on smoothly.
2. `\kappa_\Omega(n_B)\to -\kappa_\infty` at high density, so the residual sector does not collapse exponentially fast.
3. The asymptotic behavior is power-law rather than runaway, which makes the correction substantially easier to reconcile with causal reference EOS models.

The integrated residual mass is

```math
M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_B)
=
M_{\Omega,\mathrm{ref}}^{(N)}
\left[
\frac{1+\left(n_B/n_0\right)^\nu}{1+\left(n_{\mathrm{ref}}/n_0\right)^\nu}
\right]^{-\kappa_\infty/\nu},
```

where `M_{\Omega,\mathrm{ref}}^{(N)}\equiv M_\Omega^{(N)}(n_{\mathrm{ref}})`.

For a first phenomenological pass, one may take `n_{\mathrm{ref}}\sim n_0` and `M_{\Omega,\mathrm{ref}}^{(N)}\approx 0.86\ \mathrm{GeV}`, consistent with the vacuum estimate extracted above from the nucleon sigma terms.

No standalone two-parameter ansatz can guarantee `c_s^2\le 1` and `M_{\max}\ge 2M_\odot` independently of the chosen reference EOS, because those are properties of the full stellar solution rather than of `\kappa_\Omega` alone. What this bounded form does guarantee is the absence of runaway exponential softening from the `\Omega` sector itself. In practice, the intended use is therefore clear: start from a causal `2M_\odot`-compatible reference EOS, add the bounded `\Omega` correction above, and then verify the allowed region of `(\kappa_\infty,\nu)` by computing `c_s^2`, solving the TOV equations, and checking `M_{\max}`, radii, and tidal deformabilities.

## 8. What this note does not claim

To keep the note scientifically clean, the following non-claims are essential.

1. This is not a new theory of gravity.
2. This is not a derivation of mass from first principles.
3. `M_\Omega^{(H)}` is not claimed to be a unique scheme-independent operator invariant, conserved charge, or fundamental observable beyond the EFT context in which it is defined.
4. The note does not solve the cosmological constant problem.
5. The note does not provide a UV completion of QCD or of the Standard Model.

The only claim is narrower:

this residual-mass language can be turned into a physically meaningful EFT formalism if it is anchored to the nonperturbative QCD origin of hadron mass.

---

## 9. The real research program

If one wants this direction to become real theoretical physics rather than elegant language, the next tasks are clear.

1. Compute `f_\Omega^{(N)}` and `f_{\mathrm{anom}}^{(N)}` using accepted sigma terms and lattice-QCD decomposition data.
2. Determine whether `f_\Omega^{(H)}` has any near-universal behavior across light hadrons once flavor-set and scheme conventions are fixed.
3. Test whether `\kappa_\Omega(n_B)` can be embedded in a controlled neutron-star EOS model.
4. Explore whether a genuine dynamical mechanism can be written in which nonperturbative infrared QCD structure produces hadron mass nontrivially, rather than only being used as bookkeeping.

Without one of these steps, the framework remains conceptual. With them, it could mature into a useful effective language for emergent mass.

---

## 10. Conclusion

The most promising scientific future for this framework is not as a new theory of gravity, but as a QCD-focused EFT note about emergent hadron mass.

The main physically meaningful quantity is not an abstract subtracted background energy, but the non-current-mass share of a hadron:

```math
M_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F] = M_H - \sum_{q\in\mathcal F} \sigma_{qH},
\qquad
f_\Omega^{(H)}[\mathcal F] = 1 - \frac{1}{M_H}\sum_{q\in\mathcal F} \sigma_{qH}.
```

These formulas do not by themselves prove a new theory. What they do provide is an EFT-level handle tied to standard QCD physics: confinement, condensates, gluonic energy, and the trace anomaly. Their role is organizational: they summarize a residual non-current-mass sector, not a uniquely isolated microscopic operator sector. That is a much narrower claim than “new gravity,” but it is also much more scientifically viable.

At the semi-quantitative level, the nucleon estimate

```math
f_\Omega^{(N)} \approx 0.9
```

already captures the main physical lesson: most of the nucleon's mass is not explicit current-quark mass, but nonperturbative QCD energy.
